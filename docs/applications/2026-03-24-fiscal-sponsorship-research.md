## 1. Types of Fiscal Sponsorship

Gregory L. Colvin's definitive text *Fiscal Sponsorship: 6 Ways To Do It Right* (3rd edition, 2019) defines six models (A through F) plus a newer Model L. The two most commonly used are Model A and Model C.

### Model A: Comprehensive / Direct Fiscal Sponsorship

- **Structure:** The project becomes a program of the fiscal sponsor. No separate legal entity exists for the project.
- **IP Ownership:** The sponsor owns all project assets, including intellectual property, copyrights, trademarks, and domain names.
- **Fund Flow:** Sponsor receives and directly disburses all funds, pays employees and vendors.
- **Administrative Burden:** Minimal for the project — sponsor handles accounting, HR, payroll, benefits, insurance, tax compliance, legal, and operations.
- **Control:** Sponsor retains discretion and control over use of funds per IRS requirements.
- **Best For:** Early-stage projects without operational infrastructure, projects that want to focus purely on mission/impact and outsource everything else.
- **Exit:** Well-drafted agreements provide that IP and all assets transfer with the project when it spins off to its own 501(c)(3). The sponsor typically holds back some cash for a period to cover unknown liabilities.

### Model C: Pre-Approved Grant Relationship

- **Structure:** The project is a separate legal entity (can be an LLC, unincorporated association, or another nonprofit). The sponsor acts as a grantor, receiving charitable contributions and re-granting them to the project.
- **IP Ownership:** The project retains its own IP since it is a separate entity.
- **Fund Flow:** Sponsor receives donations, then re-grants to project entity. Project handles its own expenses.
- **Administrative Burden:** Higher for the project — it must manage its own tax reporting, liability, and operations.
- **Control:** Sponsor has limited management role; the project operates independently.
- **Best For:** Projects with existing operational capacity that want to maintain independence. Projects that want to keep their own IP. Projects planning to eventually spin off (the cleanest path since the project already exists as a separate entity).
- **Exit:** Simpler than Model A since the project is already a separate legal entity. Just terminate the grant relationship.

### Other Models (B, D, E, F, L)

- **Model B:** Independent Contractor — sponsor treats project leader as an independent contractor.
- **Model D:** Group Exemption — sponsor's central organization extends exemption to subordinate organizations.
- **Model E:** Supporting Organization — project becomes a supporting organization under Section 509(a)(3).
- **Model F:** Technical Assistance — sponsor provides services to the project without handling funds.
- **Model L (new in 3rd edition):** Sponsored LLC — a limited liability company structure. An LLC can elect to be treated as a corporation for federal tax purposes by filing Form 8832, and can even file Form 1023 for its own 501(c)(3) status.

### Which Model Is Best for ORGANVM?

**Model C is the strongest candidate** for a project that will eventually spin off into its own entity, because:
1. ORGANVM retains ownership of all intellectual property
2. ORGANVM can exist as its own legal entity (LLC or nonprofit) from day one
3. The transition to independence is cleaner — no need to negotiate IP transfer
4. Commercial activities can continue in a separate entity
5. The project maintains operational independence

**However, Model A may be better if:**
- ORGANVM needs maximum administrative support during the early phase
- The project lacks capacity for its own tax reporting and compliance
- The sponsor's services (HR, payroll, insurance) are critical and the project can't provide them independently

**A hybrid approach is possible:** Start with Model A for full support, negotiate strong exit terms including IP transfer, then transition to independence within 18-36 months.

## 4. Legal Considerations

### Can a Fiscally Sponsored Project Simultaneously Operate an LLC?

**Yes, with important constraints:**

- Under **Model C**, the project IS a separate legal entity (can be an LLC), and the fiscal sponsor simply re-grants charitable contributions. This is the cleanest structure for a dual entity.
- Under **Model A**, the project is a program of the sponsor. Operating a separate LLC alongside requires careful structuring to avoid private benefit issues.
- The IRS allows a 501(c)(3) to fiscally sponsor activities carried out by a for-profit entity, **provided the activities are sufficiently charitable** and align with the sponsor's mission.
- An LLC can elect to be treated as a corporation (Form 8832) or even apply for its own 501(c)(3) status (Form 1023).
- **Model L** (from Colvin's 3rd edition) specifically addresses sponsored LLCs as a fiscal sponsorship structure.

### IP Licensing Between Nonprofit Arm and Commercial Arm

- Open source licensing (MIT, Apache 2.0, GPL, etc.) naturally allows both nonprofit and commercial use
- The nonprofit arm can hold copyright and license it to the commercial arm under specific terms
- **Dual licensing** is a common model: open source license for community use, proprietary license for commercial use
- License fees from commercial arm to nonprofit arm must be at **fair market value** — not inflated (private benefit) or deflated (inurement)
- The arrangement must be **arm's-length** even if the same people control both entities

### IRS Rules on Private Benefit and Inurement

**Private Benefit:**
- A 501(c)(3) must not be organized or operated for the benefit of private interests
- Benefits to anyone other than intended charitable beneficiaries can jeopardize tax-exempt status
- The "private benefit" doctrine is broader than "inurement" — it covers benefits to ANY private party, not just insiders
- Commercial activities are permissible if they are "substantially related" to the exempt purpose, or if they are conducted as an insubstantial part of activities (with unrelated business income tax paid)

**Inurement:**
- No part of net earnings may inure to the benefit of any private shareholder or individual (insiders)
- Insiders include founders, directors, officers, key employees, and their families
- Excessive compensation, below-market transactions, and lavish benefits all constitute inurement
- **Intermediate sanctions** (Section 4958): First penalty is 20% of the transaction value; failure to correct results in 200% penalty

### Handling Commercial Revenue Within Fiscal Sponsorship

- Revenue from commercial products that are "substantially related" to the charitable mission is exempt from unrelated business income tax (UBIT)
- Revenue from activities NOT related to the mission is subject to UBIT
- If commercial revenue becomes a substantial portion of total revenue, it can jeopardize the organization's tax-exempt status
- **Best practice:** Keep commercial activities in a separate LLC and have the nonprofit license IP to the LLC at fair market value. The LLC pays licensing fees to the nonprofit, which are treated as program-related revenue.

### Recommended Legal Structure for ORGANVM

```
ORGANVM Foundation (501(c)(3) or fiscally sponsored project)
  |
  |-- Holds IP for open source components
  |-- Receives grants, donations, sponsorships
  |-- Funds research, education, community programs (Organs I, IV, V, VI, VII)
  |
  |-- Licenses IP to -->  ORGANVM LLC (commercial entity)
                            |
                            |-- Develops and sells commercial products (Organ III)
                            |-- Pays licensing fees back to Foundation
                            |-- Pays market-rate for any shared resources
```

## 5. Case Studies

### The Carpentries: NumFOCUS to Community Initiatives to Independent 501(c)(3)

**Timeline:**
- **2014:** Software Carpentry joins NumFOCUS as fiscally sponsored project
- **2015:** Data Carpentry joins NumFOCUS
- **2017:** Merger discussions begin; decision to merge approved
- **January 2018:** Formal merger into "The Carpentries." Moved from NumFOCUS to Community Initiatives for fiscal sponsorship (better service alignment). Became NumFOCUS Community Alliance Member.
- **April 2020:** Executive Director begins exploring independent nonprofit status
- **2020-2023:** Three years of streamlining operations and planning roadmap
- **August 2023:** Public announcement of transition to independence
- **February 1, 2025:** Completed transition to independent 501(c)(3)
- **February 2026:** Published one-year retrospective

**Duration Under Fiscal Sponsorship:** ~11 years total (4 years NumFOCUS, 7 years Community Initiatives)

**Why They Left:**
- Need for greater decision-making autonomy (e.g., ability to decline grants whose conditions conflicted with values)
- Modest cost savings compared to fiscal sponsorship fees
- Better access to real-time financial information
- More flexible employment policies for global workforce

**Key Lessons:**
- Transition planning took 3+ years from initial exploration to completion
- Required robust governance, financial systems, and board development
- Independence provided both financial and values-alignment benefits

### Git: Joined Software Freedom Conservancy

**Why:** Financial mismanagement of Google Summer of Code funds; needed proper accounting and tax management.
**Outcome:** Stayed under Conservancy; uses only the services needed without imposed opinions on licenses or development process.
**Key Lesson:** Fiscal sponsorship can be lightweight — use only the services you need.

### Apache Software Foundation Incubator (Analogous Model)

While not traditional fiscal sponsorship, the ASF Incubator functions similarly:

- **Apache Flink:** Entered incubator April 2014, graduated December 2014 (8 months). Entered with well-organized diverse community.
- **Apache Iceberg:** Entered November 2018, graduated May 2020 (18 months). Created at Netflix, diversified contributors from Apple, Alibaba, Adobe, LinkedIn.
- **Apache Superset:** Entered December 2010, graduated November 2020 (10 years!). Recovered from early stagnation through community diversification.
- **Apache Pinot:** Entered October 2018, graduated May 2021 (2.5 years).

**Key Lesson:** Duration under sponsorship varies wildly. Projects with strong communities can graduate in months; others take years. The Superset example shows that even a decade of incubation can result in a healthy project.

### Open Referral: Aspiration Tech

**Why:** Needed fiscal sponsorship from an organization with strong track record in open technology.
**Outcome:** Successfully operating under Aspiration's sponsorship.
**Key Lesson:** Aspiration has specific expertise in nurturing open technology projects.

### Open Collective Foundation: A Cautionary Tale

**What happened:** OCF dissolved effective December 31, 2024 after rapid COVID-era growth without adequate infrastructure. Business model was unsustainable.
**Impact:** 600+ collectives had to close or transfer. Staged dissolution gave projects months to transition.
**Key Lesson:** Evaluate the financial health and sustainability of your fiscal sponsor. Ask about their own funding model and long-term viability.

### General Spin-Off Process (from research)

**Typical Timeline:** 3-5 years under fiscal sponsorship before spinning off
**Transition Window:** 60-120 days for the actual spin-out process
**Incubation Phase:** 0-18 months of building program and track record

**Step-by-Step Spin-Off:**
1. Notify fiscal sponsor of intent to terminate
2. Incorporate new nonprofit and obtain EIN
3. Develop governance structures (board of 3-7 members minimum)
4. File Form 1023 or 1023-EZ with IRS
5. Complete state charitable registrations
6. Negotiate written asset transfer agreement (cash, IP, trademarks, domains, data)
7. Set up independent financial infrastructure (banking, accounting, payroll, insurance)
8. Coordinate with funders on grant transfers
9. Draft joint donor announcement
10. Execute transfer and begin independent operations

**Critical Pitfalls:**
- Vague exit terms create costly disputes
- Underestimating admin costs of independence
- Grant gaps when funders cannot transfer mid-cycle
- Data/donor list ownership disputes
- New nonprofit should NOT have the exact same name as the FSP (may be considered a fictitious business name of the sponsor)
